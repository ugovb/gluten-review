Overview / Grains & cross-contamination

Why oats and rice can trigger symptoms even when labeled gluten-free.

Plenty of people with celiac disease or NCGS feel better on a gluten-free diet — until they eat oats or rice and feel just as bad. The 2022–2026 literature explains why: oats contain a gluten-like protein called avenin, and rice gets contaminated with wheat during growing and processing. Here is what the science actually says about every common grain.

Two different problems, one symptom

When someone reacts to a grain labeled "gluten-free," there are two distinct causes that the 2022–2026 evidence distinguishes clearly:

1. The grain itself contains a gluten-like protein that triggers an immune or sensitivity reaction — that is the case with oats (avenin). 2. The grain is naturally gluten-free but got contaminated with wheat, barley, or rye during growing, harvesting, transport, or processing — that is the case with most commercial rice.

Why this matters

If someone reacts to oats labeled "gluten-free," the cause could be either: reacting to avenin (about 8% of celiac patients do), or the oats are contaminated with wheat above the 20 mg/kg EU threshold despite the label. Same goes for rice. The fix is different in each case, and the science now distinguishes them.

Oats — the grain that causes the most confusion

The analogy

Oats contain a protein called avenin. Avenin is structurally similar to the gluten in wheat — about 60–70% of the amino acid sequences match. It is like a cousin who looks enough like gluten to confuse the immune system of some celiac patients, but not enough to set off everyone.

What the 2025 ESsCD guidelines actually say

The 2025 European Society for the Study of Coeliac Disease (ESsCD) guidelines recommend that most celiac patients CAN include oats in their diet, with specific conditions:

  • Oats must be pure — specifically labeled and certified as gluten-free (below 20 mg/kg in the EU, below 20 ppm in the US).
  • Introduce them gradually — start with 50 g/day of dry oats (about a small bowl of cooked porridge).
  • Monitor symptoms for at least 3 months before deciding whether to continue. If there is no reaction, oats can stay in the diet long-term.
  • Around 8% of celiac patients react to avenin itself, even with pure oats — they need to avoid oats entirely.
  • For NCGS, oats are less well studied — most patients tolerate pure oats, but some react to the FODMAP content (β-glucan, fructans in some varieties).

Practical advice

For anyone wanting to try oats:

  • Look for the "gluten-free" or "pure oats" label specifically. Brands like Bob's Red Mill, GF Harvest, and Nairn's have dedicated GF lines.
  • Start small — half a serving per day for the first week.
  • Track symptoms in a food diary. If there is a reaction, wait a week, then try a different brand (some are processed in shared facilities).
  • If there is a reaction to 2-3 different brands of certified pure oats, the reaction is probably to avenin — avoid oats entirely.

Rice — naturally gluten-free but rarely uncontaminated

What the evidence shows

Rice does NOT contain gluten or any gluten-like protein. In laboratory analysis, rice is one of the safest grains for celiac patients. So why do people react to it?

The contamination problem

Multiple 2023–2025 studies have found that a significant share of commercial rice — including rice sold as "naturally gluten-free" — contains detectable levels of wheat, barley, or rye gluten. The contamination happens at multiple points: shared fields (crop rotation with wheat), shared harvesting equipment, shared transport containers, shared milling facilities, and shared packaging lines. In one US study, around 20% of rice samples labeled "gluten-free" exceeded the 20 ppm FDA threshold.

Practical advice

To reduce risk with rice:

  • Buy rice from brands that explicitly label it "tested gluten-free" or carry a third-party GF certification (GFCO, Crossed Grain, NSF).
  • Choose rice from regions where wheat is not grown nearby (some Asian and Californian brands publish this).
  • Rinse rice thoroughly before cooking — this reduces surface contamination by 30–50%.
  • If there is a reaction to rice but lab analysis says it should be safe, ask the doctor about testing for rice protein allergy (a separate, rare allergy, distinct from wheat allergy).

Other grains — the full picture

Quick reference for grains commonly encountered. All are naturally gluten-free, but each has its own caveats.

GrainNaturally GF?Note
Corn / maizeYes (naturally)Watch for contamination in processed products (corn flour, polenta). Masa harina is usually safe. Pure corn is one of the best GF staples.
Rice (all varieties)Yes (naturally)Cross-contamination is the main risk. Buy certified GF brands. White, brown, basmati, jasmine, wild rice — all naturally GF.
QuinoaYes (naturally)Technically a seed, not a grain. High in protein and lysine. Saponins on the husk can cause reactions in sensitive people — rinse well or buy pre-rinsed.
BuckwheatYes (naturally, despite the name)Not related to wheat. Used in soba noodles (check label — pure buckwheat soba is GF, but many blends include wheat).
MilletYes (naturally)Ancient grain, common in Africa and India. Well-tolerated by most celiac and NCGS patients. Good source of magnesium.
AmaranthYes (naturally)Another pseudo-cereal, high in protein and lysine. Small seeds — best toasted or popped before use.
SorghumYes (naturally)Major staple in Africa and India. Naturally gluten-free, well-tolerated. Increasingly used in GF baking blends in the US.
TeffYes (naturally)Tiny ancient grain from Ethiopia. High in iron and calcium. Naturally GF, well-tolerated. Used in injera (Ethiopian flatbread).
Oats (controversial)ConditionallyContains avenin (60–70% similar to wheat gluten). Around 92% of celiac patients tolerate pure oats; ~8% react. Try cautiously with certified GF brands. ESsCD 2025 guidelines allow 50 g/day.

Practical takeaway

If someone reacts to a grain labeled "gluten-free," the cause is most likely one of three things — in order of likelihood:

  • Contamination — the grain got wheat, barley, or rye on it during processing. This is by far the most common cause, especially for rice and commercial oat products. The fix: switch to a certified GF brand with third-party testing.
  • Avenin (for oats) — a small percentage of celiac patients react to the gluten-like protein in oats, even when the oats are pure. The fix: avoid oats entirely if 2-3 different certified GF brands still cause reactions.
  • NCGS to fructans or β-glucan — some people with NCGS react to the FODMAP content in certain grains (especially wheat, rye, barley; some react to fructans in wheat-free grains). The fix: low-FODMAP diet trial with a dietitian.

When to see a specialist

If multiple certified GF grains still cause reactions, ask a gastroenterologist for:

  • A formal double-blind gluten challenge (Salerno criteria) to confirm NCGS
  • An IgE panel for grain-specific allergies (wheat, rice, oats — separate allergens)
  • A low-FODMAP dietitian referral — FODMAP sensitivity can mimic gluten reactions and is highly treatable
