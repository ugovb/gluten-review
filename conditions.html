Overview / The 3 conditions

Celiac, NCGS, and wheat allergy — the three things that look the same but aren't.

Same trigger, same broad symptom list, but three fundamentally different mechanisms. Treating them as interchangeable is the most common mistake — including in clinical practice.

Condition 1 of 3

Celiac disease

Autoimmune. Inflammatory. Biopsy-confirmed. The most medically serious of the three, and the one you have already ruled out for yourself.

Mechanism

Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder where the immune system mistakenly identifies gluten proteins (specifically gliadin peptides from wheat, plus related proteins in rye and barley) as threats. In genetically susceptible individuals — those carrying HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 haplotypes — this triggers an adaptive immune response that damages the villi lining the small intestine.

The 2022 Lancet review by Catassi [1] describes the resulting enteropathy as "a complex immune response to gluten proteins, including both adaptive and innate mechanisms." In plain language: T-cells go on the attack, inflammation builds, the intestinal brush border flattens, and nutrient absorption collapses.

How it's diagnosed

The 2025 European Society for the Study of Coeliac Disease (ESsCD) guidelines [2][3] lay out a tiered approach:

  1. First-line serology: IgA anti-tissue transglutaminase 2 (anti-TG2). If you ate gluten normally before the test, a negative result has high negative predictive value. (This is what you had done.)
  2. High-titre shortcut (2025 update): Adults with anti-TG2 ≥10× upper limit of normal can now be diagnosed without biopsy, in selected cases.
  3. Confirmatory biopsy (when serology is borderline): At least four duodenal samples, with bulb biopsies conditionally included.
  4. HLA typing: Recommended for diagnostic clarification in select cases — a negative HLA-DQ2/DQ8 essentially rules out celiac.

Why your negative antibody test is reliable

Anti-tTG IgA has ~95–98% sensitivity for active celiac disease when the patient is on a gluten-containing diet. False negatives do occur — IgA deficiency (affecting ~1 in 500 people), early disease, very recent gluten reduction — but for the typical case, a negative result is enough to rule out celiac without further testing.

Treatment

Lifelong strict gluten-free diet. No pharmacological therapy is yet approved for celiac disease in the EU, although multiple candidates (latiglutenase, TAK-227, and various tolerance-inducing biologics [4]) are in trials as of 2026. The Wall 2024 review [5] emphasizes that a registered dietitian's guidance is mandatory — gluten is ubiquitous and contamination is the most common cause of persistent symptoms even in diagnosed patients.

Comorbidities

The 2025 Nature Reviews Gastroenterology paper by Ludvigsson [6] reviews the long list: osteoporosis, intestinal lymphoma, other autoimmune conditions (type 1 diabetes, thyroid disease), reproductive complications, and increased all-cause mortality if untreated.

Condition 2 of 3

Non-celiac gluten / wheat sensitivity (NCGS / NCWS)

The contested middle category. Real symptoms, contested mechanism, no biomarker, and a 2024–2026 debate about whether "gluten" is even the right word.

Definition

NCGS is what you call it when someone has intestinal or extra-intestinal symptoms triggered by gluten-containing foods, after celiac disease and wheat allergy have been ruled out. The 2025 Lancet review by Biesiekierski [7] — one of the field's leading voices — describes it as a "diagnosis of exclusion" with "the absence of biomarkers, significant overlap with disorders of gut-brain interaction, and methodological challenges in dietary evaluation."

What might actually be causing it

This is the most interesting — and most debated — part of the 2022–2026 literature:

  • Gluten itself — the original hypothesis, still on the table but weakening.
  • Other wheat proteins (ATIs — amylase-trypsin inhibitors) — activate innate immune cells in the gut. Highlighted in Catassi 2024 [8] and Bektaş 2026 [9].
  • Fructans — a FODMAP carbohydrate in wheat, rye, and barley. The 2024 de Graaf study [10] and Fernandes Dias 2024 meta-analysis [11] show low-FODMAP diets improve symptoms in many NCGS self-reporters.
  • Nocebo effect — the expectation of symptoms after gluten is itself a powerful trigger. The de Graaf study [10] showed this clearly.
  • Disorders of gut-brain interaction (DGBI) — what used to be called IBS. Khan 2025 [12] and others now argue "NCGS is simply a form of irritable bowel syndrome."

This is why many 2024–2026 reviews are pushing the rename to non-celiac wheat sensitivity (NCWS): it doesn't lock in gluten as the trigger prematurely.

Diagnosis — the Salerno experts' criteria

The 2015 Salerno experts' consensus remains the most rigorous working framework, though its validity is "contradictory" per the Spanish Expósito Miranda 2023 review [13]. The protocol:

  1. Symptom evaluation on a gluten-containing diet (GCD).
  2. Six weeks on a gluten-free diet (GFD) — assess symptom response.
  3. Double-blind, placebo-controlled gluten challenge (one week each of gluten and placebo, separated by a washout).
  4. Symptom re-evaluation. Symptoms should reappear with gluten, not placebo, with a 30% threshold for clinical significance.

Correa-Ventura 2025 [14] found only 6 of 18 NCGS studies in their systematic review actually applied the Salerno protocol — most NCGS research in the literature is methodologically weak.

Prevalence

Self-reported by ~10% of adults worldwide [7]. But in double-blind challenge studies, only 16–30% of self-reporters actually react to gluten specifically — the rest are reacting to expectation (nocebo), FODMAPs, or something else entirely. This is one of the most important numbers in the field.

The Vignoli 2025 metabolomics study

A small but rigorous 2025 study [15] used NMR-based metabolomics to compare serum from celiac, potential-celiac, and NCGS patients. The model could distinguish CD from NCGS with AUC 0.90 — strong discrimination — pointing to gut microbiota alterations in NCGS. This is the most promising lead toward a future biomarker, but it's a pilot, not a clinical test yet.

Condition 3 of 3

Wheat allergy

An allergic reaction, full stop. Same mechanism as a peanut or shellfish allergy — and the easiest of the three to test for.

Mechanism

Wheat allergy is an IgE-mediated (sometimes non-IgE) hypersensitivity to wheat proteins. The Størdal 2025 review [16] describes "rapid IgE-mediated reactions characterized by specific IgE antibodies in conjunction with symptoms originating especially from the respiratory and gastrointestinal tract." The major allergens are gliadins (especially ω-5-gliadin) and glutenins.

Subtypes

Srisuwatchari 2025 [17] lays out the clinical varieties:

  • Classical IgE-mediated wheat allergy — hives, anaphylaxis, vomiting, within minutes to hours. Most common in children; often outgrown by adolescence.
  • Wheat-dependent exercise-induced anaphylaxis (WDEIA) — symptoms only when wheat is consumed and the patient exercises within a few hours. ω-5-gliadin is the main allergen.
  • Non-IgE-mediated wheat allergy — less well-defined, delayed onset, diagnostically harder. Diagnosed by exclusion + dietary challenge.
  • Hydrolyzed wheat protein anaphylaxis — increasingly reported, often from cosmetic products containing HWP.

Prevalence

Liu 2023's global meta-analysis [18] found wheat allergy affects about 0.6% of the population (self-reported), with the highest rates in Northern Europe and parts of Asia. It is more common in children than adults and is one of the more common food allergies to be outgrown.

How it's diagnosed

  1. Skin prick test (SPT) — quick, cheap, but variable specificity.
  2. Specific IgE (sIgE) blood test — wheat sIgE, plus component-resolved diagnostics (CRD) for ω-5-gliadin, LTP, etc.
  3. Basophil activation test (BAT) — emerging, more specific.
  4. Oral food challenge (OFC) — the gold standard but carries anaphylaxis risk, done in clinical settings.

Treatment

Strict wheat avoidance (not necessarily gluten avoidance — rye and barley are usually fine unless there's cross-reactivity). Emergency plan with an epinephrine auto-injector if there's a history of anaphylaxis.

A different category: alpha-gal syndrome

Worth flagging: a 2026 review by Awosika [19] shows that alpha-gal syndrome (a tick-borne IgE reaction to galactose-α-1,3-galactose) is frequently misdiagnosed as NCGS or lactose intolerance because of overlapping GI symptoms. The distinguishing feature is a 2–6 hour delay between eating mammalian meat and symptom onset. If your "gluten" symptoms include delayed reactions after eating beef, pork, or lamb, mention this to your doctor.

Putting them side by side

How doctors tell the three apart.

This is the workflow a gastroenterologist or allergist would actually follow when a patient presents with "I feel bad after gluten."

Feature Celiac disease NCGS / NCWS Wheat allergy
Mechanism Autoimmune (T-cell mediated) Unknown / multi-factorial (innate immunity, FODMAPs, nocebo) IgE-mediated (type I hypersensitivity)
Prevalence ~1% Self-report ~10%; confirmed 1–6% ~0.6% (higher in children)
Key diagnostic test Anti-tTG IgA + duodenal biopsy Salerno criteria (DBPC gluten challenge) Skin prick test, specific IgE, oral food challenge
Biomarker Yes (anti-tTG, anti-EMA, anti-DGP) None validated Yes (sIgE wheat, ω-5-gliadin)
Genetics HLA-DQ2 / DQ8 (required but not sufficient) No consistent genetic profile Atopic background
Triggers Wheat, rye, barley (gluten) Wheat, sometimes other grains; FODMAPs play a role Wheat only (rye/barley usually OK)
Treatment Lifelong strict GFD GFD or low-FODMAP, individualized Wheat avoidance + epi-pen if anaphylaxis history

What this means for you, practically

Since your anti-tTG was negative and you weren't IgA-deficient or on a gluten-reduced diet at the time, celiac is off the table. The remaining diagnostic workup is to distinguish NCGS/NCWS from wheat allergy — and the way to do that is an allergy workup (specific IgE + skin prick test for wheat). If those are negative, the rest is a clinical NCGS/NCWS workup using the Salerno or similar criteria.
