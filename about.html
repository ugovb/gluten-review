How this review was built.

A transparent account of the search strategy, the databases queried, and the verification gates every citation passed through.

The pipeline

Search → verify → deduplicate → score → cite.

Built using the `literature-review` and `doi-verify` skills with PubMed (NCBI E-utilities), OpenAlex, and the Crossref REST API.

Step 1 — Broad PubMed search

A combined Boolean query covering all three conditions, filtered to English/Spanish, publication dates 2022/01/01 to 2026/12/31:

"Celiac Disease"[Mesh] OR "non-celiac gluten sensitivity"[tiab] OR "NCGS"[tiab] OR "gluten sensitivity"[tiab] OR "gluten intolerance"[tiab] OR "Wheat Hypersensitivity"[Mesh] OR "wheat allergy"[tiab] OR "coeliac disease"[tiab] AND ("2022/01/01"[PDAT] : "2026/12/31"[PDAT])

This returned 2,772 results. The top 80 by PubMed's relevance ranking were fetched in full (XML efetch), plus 64 additional papers from targeted sub-queries (Spain, diagnostic criteria, wheat allergy reviews, nocebo/FODMAP).

Step 2 — Targeted sub-queries

Five targeted Boolean queries were added to capture angles the broad query would miss:

  • Spain + celiac / NCGS — 156 results
  • Spain + gluten-free regulation / AESAN / FACE — 8 results
  • NCGS + diagnostic criteria / Salerno — 20 results
  • Wheat allergy review filter — 27 results
  • NCGS + nocebo / expectancy / FODMAP — 21 results

Step 3 — OpenAlex pass

OpenAlex's full-text search is permissive and noisy for narrow clinical topics, so it was used for completeness checks rather than as the primary source. The bulk of evidence came from PubMed MeSH-controlled indexing, which is the right tool for biomedical queries.

Step 4 — Deduplication

Merged by PMID, then deduplicated by canonical DOI (lowercased, Crossref-normalized). Final: 142 unique DOIs + 2 without DOI.

Step 5 — DOI verification (the hallucination gate)

Every DOI was batch-resolved through the Crossref REST API (the canonical DOI metadata registry). Each candidate was checked for:

  • DOI returns HTTP 200 with metadata (vs. 404 / hallucinated)
  • Type is journal-article, proceedings-article, or book-chapter — preprints (posted-content) excluded unless explicitly intentional
  • Year matches the claim (within ±1 for in-press)
  • Title matches what the LLM would have cited (verified during the writing step)
  • is-retracted is false

Result: 140/142 DOIs resolved successfully. The two failures (one Chinese-language domestic journal, one non-journal document) were excluded from the citation set.

Step 6 — Relevance scoring

The verified 140 papers were scored on a weighted rubric for relevance to Ugo's question (CD vs NCGS vs wheat allergy, plus Spain angle):

  • NCGS / gluten-sensitivity keywords: +3 per match
  • Wheat-allergy keywords: +2 per match
  • Spain / Spanish / AESAN / FACE keywords: +5 per match (unique angle)
  • Differential diagnosis / Salerno / guideline / consensus: +2 per match
  • Publication type = Review / Systematic Review / Meta-Analysis / Practice Guideline: +4
  • High-impact journal (Lancet, Nature, Gut, Gastroenterology, BMJ, JAMA, etc.): +2

The top 25 by score, plus the highest-impact works (Lancet, Nature Reviews, ESsCD guidelines), were prioritized for direct abstract review and citation in this site.

Step 7 — Reading + writing

Abstracts were read directly from PubMed efetch XML (not abstracts of abstracts). Key claims in the site text are quoted from the abstracts, with [N] citations linking to the verified DOI on the evidence page.

What's deliberately NOT in this review

  • Pre-2022 papers — the question was specifically about the current literature. Earlier landmark works (Sapone 2012 NCGS consensus, etc.) are not cited.
  • Non-peer-reviewed sources — no wellness blogs, no manufacturer claims, no news articles.
  • Pirate / Sci-Hub copies — no paper is cited from a non-official source. Each DOI is open, PMC-archived, or paywalled (we cite the DOI regardless of access).
  • Self-reporting survey data without biological verification — the gluten-free market data (USD 20.1B in 2021) is in the Bektaş 2026 paper for context only; it's not used to make medical claims.
  • Speculative "non-celiac" claims without blinded challenge data — the 16–30% number (Biesiekierski 2025) is the conservative figure based on controlled studies, not self-report.
Important

What this review is not.

Not medical advice

This is a literature review for educational purposes. It is not a diagnostic tool, not a treatment recommendation, and not a substitute for consultation with a gastroenterologist, allergist, or registered dietitian. The decision to start, continue, or stop a gluten-free diet should be made with a healthcare professional who knows your full medical history.

Not a substitute for the actual papers

Every claim here is paraphrased from or directly quoted from the cited abstracts. The full papers contain nuance (statistical methods, sample sizes, exclusion criteria, conflict-of-interest disclosures) that this site cannot reproduce. If a claim matters to you, click through to the DOI and read the original.

Built on a date cutoff

The literature search was completed on the day this site was generated. Medicine moves — new papers appear weekly, and the 2026 picture will look different from the 2027 picture. If a question matters, check the literature again in six months.
