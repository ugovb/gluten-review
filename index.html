The short answer

Three completely different things get called "gluten problems".

When someone says gluten makes them sick, it could mean three medically distinct conditions, and they have different mechanisms, different tests, and different treatments. Most confusion happens because the symptoms look similar, and the dietary advice looks the same (avoid gluten), so people assume it's all one thing.

It isn't. Here is the 2026 consensus from the European Society for the Study of Coeliac Disease [1], the Lancet [2][3], and a five-year narrative review in Nutrients [4]:

Celiac Disease (CD)

An autoimmune attack

  • What it is. Your immune system mistakes gluten for a threat and damages your small intestine.
  • How common. ~1% of the population worldwide.
  • How it's diagnosed. Blood antibody test (anti-tTG IgA) + intestinal biopsy. You already had the antibody test and it was negative — that rules CD out very reliably.
  • Treatment. Strict, lifelong gluten-free diet.
Non-Celiac Gluten / Wheat Sensitivity (NCGS / NCWS)

Real symptoms, unclear mechanism

  • What it is. Symptoms after eating gluten/wheat, with CD and wheat allergy ruled out. The trigger is debated — gluten, other wheat proteins (ATIs), or FODMAPs.
  • How common. Self-reported by ~10% of adults; controlled studies confirm 16–30% of self-reporters react to gluten specifically.
  • How it's diagnosed. Diagnosis of exclusion — no biomarker. Uses the Salerno experts' criteria: symptoms + double-blind gluten challenge + symptom response.
  • Treatment. Gluten-free or low-FODMAP diet, individualized.
Wheat Allergy (WA)

A classic allergic reaction

  • What it is. IgE-mediated (or sometimes non-IgE) immune reaction to wheat proteins — same kind of mechanism as a peanut or shellfish allergy.
  • How common. ~0.6% globally; higher in children, often outgrown.
  • How it's diagnosed. Skin prick test, specific IgE blood test, oral food challenge.
  • Treatment. Wheat avoidance + emergency plan for anaphylaxis.

Given your antibody-negative result

Celiac disease is effectively ruled out by a negative anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-tTG) IgA test, provided you were eating gluten at the time of the test [1][2]. The remaining plausible categories are non-celiac gluten/wheat sensitivity (NCGS/NCWS) and wheat allergy — these are distinguished from each other by an allergist's workup (specific IgE, skin prick test, and possibly a food challenge).

If you have never had a wheat-specific IgE or skin prick test, that's the next step worth discussing with a doctor.

What's actually new

The 2022–2026 picture, in plain language.

1. The medical world is leaning toward renaming NCGS

Multiple 2024–2026 reviews [3][4][5] point out that "non-celiac gluten sensitivity" may be a misnomer. When you give blinded challenges to people who think they react to gluten, many of them also react to fructans (a FODMAP carbohydrate) and to other wheat proteins called ATIs (amylase-trypsin inhibitors). The newer term — non-celiac wheat sensitivity (NCWS) — is gaining traction because it doesn't pre-commit to gluten as the trigger.

2. The nocebo effect is huge in this field

A landmark 2024 randomized double-blind study [6] from Leeds, Maastricht, and Wageningen put self-reported NCGS sufferers in four groups: (a) told they'd eat gluten, ate gluten, (b) told they'd eat gluten, ate placebo, (c) told placebo, ate gluten, (d) told placebo, ate placebo. The strongest symptoms appeared in group (b) — people who expected gluten but got placebo. Symptoms in group (c) — actual gluten, no expectation — were small. This doesn't mean NCGS is fake; it means expectation strongly modulates symptoms, which is consistent with disorders of gut-brain interaction (the modern framing of IBS-like conditions).

3. The diagnostic protocol is contested

The 2015 Salerno experts' criteria require a double-blind placebo-controlled gluten challenge — but a Spanish 2023 review [7] noted contradictory results on its validity, and only 6 of 18 studies in a recent anemia/NCGS systematic review [8] actually used the Salerno protocol. The 2025 ESsCD guidelines [1] don't endorse NCGS as a standalone diagnosis in the way they endorse celiac; they treat NCGS-like symptoms as a clinical entity worth working up, not a disease with a test.

4. Spain's "no distinction" observation is regulatory, not medical

In Spain, the same EU-wide gluten-free labeling regulation applies as in France, Germany, Italy — there is no separate "celiac aisle" in supermarkets because EU Implementing Regulation 828/2014 only defines two categories: "gluten-free" (≤20 mg/kg) and "very low gluten" (≤100 mg/kg). The distinction between celiac disease, NCGS, and wheat allergy exists in the medical literature but doesn't show up on a food label — that is what you noticed, and it's by EU design, not a Spanish oversight.
