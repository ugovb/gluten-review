Overview / Spain & Regulation

Why Spain (and the whole EU) doesn't make the distinction on a food label.

Your observation is correct — there is no "celiac aisle" or "NCGS section" at the Mercadona. That's not because Spain is wrong; it's by EU regulation.

The regulatory landscape

The European Union, of which Spain is a member, governs "gluten-free" claims through Implementing Regulation (EU) No 828/2014. It defines exactly two categories of claim that can appear on a food label:

"gluten-free"

≤ 20 mg/kg

  • What it means. The product contains 20 milligrams of gluten per kilogram or less.
  • Who it's safe for. People with celiac disease (the strictest standard). Also safe for NCGS and wheat allergy.
"very low gluten"

≤ 100 mg/kg

  • What it means. Between 21 and 100 mg/kg — most celiac patients avoid this category.
  • Who it's safe for. A small number of people with celiac who tolerate trace gluten; some NCGS patients.
No claim

Standard foods

  • What it means. No regulated gluten content. Naturally gluten-free foods (rice, quinoa, legumes) don't carry the claim but are still safe.
  • Cross-contamination. Oat products, for example, must be specifically labeled "gluten-free" if contamination was controlled.

The structural reason there's no "celiac vs NCGS" distinction on labels

The EU regulation is built around a single threshold (20 mg/kg) calibrated for the most sensitive group — people with celiac disease. From a regulatory standpoint, if it's safe for the strictest case, it's safe for everyone, so no further distinction is needed.

The medical literature, by contrast, is built around mechanism and individual variation. NCGS patients have no defined tolerance threshold, no biomarker, and their symptom triggers vary widely. There is no regulatory framework that could meaningfully distinguish "NCGS-safe" from "celiac-safe" — the science doesn't yet support it.

How Spain implements this in practice

Spain's national implementation is handled by AESAN (Agencia Española de Seguridad Alimentaria y Nutrición). At the consumer-facing level, this means:

  • Supermarket shelves — There is no "celiac aisle" separate from "gluten-free" anywhere in the EU. What you find is a "sin gluten" or "sin trigo" section, governed by the same regulation as in Germany, France, or Italy. The lack of distinction is EU policy, not Spanish oversight.
  • Restaurants — Spain has no nationwide mandatory gluten-free certification for restaurants. The FACE (Federación de Asociaciones de Celíacos de España) maintains a list of partner restaurants that meet voluntary standards for celiac-safe food preparation, but this is a celiac-patients-first framework, not an NCGS one.
  • School meals — Some autonomous communities (Comunidad de Madrid, Andalucía) provide gluten-free menu options in public schools, again primarily framed around celiac disease.
  • Healthcare coverage — Spain's public system (SNS) covers celiac diagnostic workup and dietitian consultations for celiac disease, but does not typically fund NCGS-specific workups. NCGS remains a "diagnosis of exclusion" in clinical practice — you can get it, but it requires going through celiac and wheat allergy first.

What the Spanish literature contributes

Spanish researchers are active in the NCGS field. The most-cited 2023 review is the Expósito Miranda paper in Gastroenterología y Hepatología, the official journal of the Spanish Society of Gastroenterology.

Gastroenterol Hepatol · 2023 Expósito Miranda R et al.

Non-celiac gluten sensitivity: Clinical presentation, etiology and differential diagnosis

"Non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) is the latest pathology incorporated into the group of gluten-related disorders. This review addresses the evidence on its etiology, differential diagnosis and symptomatology. Although NCGS is defined by a reaction to gluten, other possible etiopathogenic mechanisms have been described, such as an inadequate response to other components of wheat or to FODMAPs, with the term non-celiac sensitivity to wheat recently being extended. There are contradictory results on the validity of the diagnostic protocol of the Salerno experts."

Citation
Expósito Miranda R et al. Gastroenterol Hepatol. 2023;46(1):40-51. doi:10.1016/j.gastrohep.2022.10.001

A handful of other Spanish-authored 2022–2026 papers on the topic: Capra 2025 on pediatric nutritional challenges, Correa-Ventura 2025 on NCGS and anemia (systematic review). Spanish clinical practice, like the rest of the EU, treats "gluten-related disorders" as one umbrella for regulatory purposes but distinguishes them carefully at the diagnostic level.

Bottom line on Spain

What changes and what doesn't

Doesn't change (regulatory): Food labels in Spain will continue to read "sin gluten" (≤20 mg/kg) or "muy bajo en gluten" (≤100 mg/kg). The EU regulatory framework treats all gluten-sensitive people as one category defined by the strictest threshold.

Does change (medical, 2022–2026): The Spanish medical literature, like the European literature overall, increasingly distinguishes NCGS from celiac at the diagnostic level — through differential diagnosis (Salerno criteria, gluten challenges), not through food labeling. If you work with a Spanish gastroenterologist in 2026, they will know the three-condition framework even if the supermarket doesn't show it.

Your observation that "in Spain there is no existing difference between celiac and gluten-intolerant people" is half right: in the supermarket and at the regulatory level, that's accurate. At the diagnostic and clinical level, the three-condition framework is the standard of care.
